\section*{Q1.}
Consider the following graph $G$:\\
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}

  % Nodes
  \node[circle, draw] (C) at (0,0) {A};
  \node[circle, draw] (D) at (3,0) {D};
  \node[circle, draw] (B) at (0,3) {B};
  \node[circle, draw] (A) at (3,3) {C};

  % Edges with weights
  \draw (B) -- node[midway, left]  {5} (C);
  \draw (B) -- node[midway, above] {2} (A);
  \draw (C) -- node[midway, below] {4} (A);
  \draw (C) -- node[midway, below] {6} (D);
  \draw (D) -- node[midway, right] {8} (A);

\end{tikzpicture}
  \end{center}
With the following MWST and MCPT's 

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}

\mwst & \mcpta & \mcptb & \mcptc & \mcptd\\
MWST & MCPT(A) & MCPT(B) & MCPT(C) & MCPT(D) 
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Since the MCPT of $G$ rooted at A,B,C \& D all differ from the MWST of $G$, the above is a concrete counter example to the below claim thus, disproving it:

\begin{Verbatim}[frame=single]
In a connected edge-weighted undirected graph with distinct edge weights and no negative-cost edges,
there always exists some vertex v such that a minimum cost path tree rooted at v is also a minimum 
weight spanning tree.
\end{Verbatim}
